# When Housing Broke — and Why It Hasn't Fixed Itself

## The Number That Summarizes Everything

In 2000, the median US home cost 3.9 times the median household income.
In 2022, it cost 6.1 times. The lender rule of thumb for a manageable
mortgage has always been 3x income. That threshold was crossed around 2003
and has never come back.

By 2023, the average family buying a median-priced home at the prevailing
mortgage rate was spending **34.7% of gross income** on the monthly payment.
Lenders consider 28% the upper limit of affordability. We are 6 points over.

---

## Why 2021-2022 Was Different From the 2006 Bubble

The 2006 housing bubble was driven by bad loans — teaser rates, no-doc
mortgages, subprime lending. The 2021-2022 surge was different and arguably
harder to fix.

Causes:
- Mortgage rates at historic lows (2.96% in 2021 — lowest ever recorded)
- Pandemic-driven demand shift: remote work freed buyers from city centers
- Supply collapsed during COVID construction shutdowns — never recovered
- Institutional investors buying single-family homes at scale
- Zoning restrictions in high-demand metros blocking new supply

The 2006 bubble burst because the loans were fraudulent. The 2021 surge
was real demand, real buyers, real prices. Prices haven't crashed —
they've plateaued at 5.5x income while rates shot up to 6.81% in 2023.

That combination is the worst of both worlds: prices didn't fall, but
rates doubled. Monthly payments went up 47% from 2021 to 2023.

---

## The Rent Trap

People locked out of homeownership are competing in the rental market —
which drove rents up sharply at the same time.

Florida's median 2BR rent: $1,620/month. Median household income: $59,227.
That's 32.8% of gross income on rent alone — before utilities, groceries,
transportation, healthcare.

HUD defines "cost burdened" as spending more than 30% of income on housing.
**12 of 50 states** have median rents that put the average household in
cost-burdened territory.

The trap: you can't buy because prices are too high and rates are high.
You can't save for a down payment because rent is eating your income.

---

## 12 States in Crisis

The following states have a price-to-income ratio above 5x,
the threshold the OECD classifies as "severely unaffordable":

Hawaii (9.6x), California (9.2x), Massachusetts (6.5x),
Colorado (7.3x), Washington (6.8x), Oregon (7.0x), Utah (6.5x),
Idaho (6.8x), Montana (6.6x), Nevada (6.3x), New Jersey (5.3x),
and Connecticut (5.0x).

These states have two things in common: supply constraints (geography, zoning,
NIMBYism) and high-income knowledge economy jobs that push up nominal prices
faster than they push up median incomes.

---

## The Iowa Exception

Iowa's price-to-income ratio is 3.0x — right at the traditional affordability
threshold. Median home price: $185,000. Median income: $62,483.

Iowa works because it doesn't have the geographic constraints of coastal states,
it never had the tech boom demand surge, and zoning is less restrictive.
It's not that Iowa fixed housing. It's that Iowa never broke it.
